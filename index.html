The problem

Roughly 50 million people live with epilepsy. A seizure missed is a life at risk.

Epileptic seizures show up in the electroencephalogram (EEG) as sudden, high‑energy bursts of electrical activity. Reading these traces by hand is slow and needs an expert neurologist. This project trains a model to do the reading automatically — turning a one‑second window of raw brain signal into a seizure / no‑seizure decision, the building block of a wearable seizure‑alert system.

Each recording is labelled 1–5. Class 1 is an active seizure; classes 2–5 are non‑seizure brain states (tumour area, healthy area, eyes closed, eyes open). We collapse this into a binary task — seizure vs. everything else — a realistic 20% / 80% class imbalance handled with class weighting.

Why CNN + LSTM

Convolutions learn the local shapes of the signal — the sharp spikes and waves of a seizure. The LSTM then models how those shapes evolve over the one‑second sequence. Together they read an EEG the way a neurologist scans a trace.

What makes the result trustworthy

A fixed random seed, a stratified train/validation/test split, and scaling fit on training data only — so no test information leaks into the model. A Random Forest baseline sets the bar the deep model must beat. Because a missed seizure is worse than a false alarm, a threshold‑tuning step lets the operating point favour recall.

Where it goes next

The honest next steps that would take this from coursework to publishable: patient‑independent splits (group by subject) to prove it generalises to new people, k‑fold cross‑validation for tighter error bars, spectral / spectrogram features, the full 5‑class problem, and export to TensorFlow Lite for on‑device seizure alerting.
